# Pure Software is Uninvestable — Naval's Verdict

A capital-allocation thesis attributed to [[naval-ravikant|Naval]] (via [[naval-apple-dead-saas-next-18-months]]): **software-only companies — those whose moat is "we built something hard" — no longer warrant venture-grade investment**, because AI coding agents have collapsed the cost and time of replication.

## The Core Claim

> "Pure software is uninvestable. Full stop."

The verdict has three layers:

1. **The economic layer** — when execution time drops from years to weeks, prior valuations were pricing in friction that no longer exists.
2. **The competitive layer** — any pure-software product is now copyable in a quarter by a 2-person team with [[skill-era|Claude Code]]-class tooling. Difficulty was the moat. Difficulty is gone.
3. **The capital layer** — VCs allocating to "we built X faster/better" are funding decaying assets. Capital flows toward genuinely uncopyable surfaces (hardware, data flywheels, network effects, distribution, vertical depth).

## What "Pure Software" Means Here

Operationally, pure software ≈ **a company whose only moat is the codebase itself**. Symptoms:

- "Our product is just better" is the honest answer to "what's your moat?"
- Roadmap is feature-by-feature differentiation against horizontal competitors.
- No data flywheel, no network effects, no integrated hardware, no owned distribution, no regulatory depth.
- Pricing is per-seat or per-feature — not outcome-anchored.
- Onboarding/UX is the sales motion, not workflow ownership.

By this definition, **most B2B SaaS companies sitting on Series A/B valuations from 2021-2023 fit the description**.

## What's NOT Pure Software (and survives)

The same source articulates the inverse moat taxonomy:

- **Distribution + audience** ([[distribution-over-product]], [[media-plus-agents-distribution]])
- **Network effects** — value grows with users, not features
- **Data flywheels** ([[data-compounding-moat]])
- **Hardware integration** — the physical world is hard, AI doesn't make chips
- **Vertical depth** ([[vertical-operator-edge]])
- **Brand / community / regulatory depth**

A "software company" with one or more of these isn't pure software — it's a software-skinned business with a real moat.

## The Math of Replication

The claim that makes this thesis concrete:

> "A 2-person team using Claude Code can now replicate 80% of most B2B SaaS products in under 90 days. Not a toy version. A working version. With proper architecture, basic security, room to scale."

The remaining 20% (custom integrations, enterprise sales motion, compliance stack) is **friction, not moat** — and friction compresses with every next-generation agent shipping each quarter.

This number isn't audited. It's a directional claim. **The thesis holds even at "60% in 6 months."**

## Connection to the Skill Era

This concept is the **capital-markets translation of the [[skill-era]] thesis**:

- Skill Era says: *value is moving from APIs (functionality) to skills (judgment)*.
- Pure-software-uninvestable says: *therefore, capital priced into difficulty-of-execution is mispriced; capital priced into uncopyable judgment / distribution / data is correctly priced*.

Same insight, two registers: one for builders, one for allocators.

## Implication for Alteramens

Every Alteramens project, present and future, gets evaluated against this filter:

> *"If this product is rebuildable in 90 days by 2 people with Claude Code, what is the moat that survives the rebuild?"*

Acceptable answers:
- **Distribution** (audience, owned channels, content authority)
- **Vertical operator edge** (specific knowledge in healthcare/accounting that 2-person teams without operator fluency can't fake)
- **Encoded judgment as compounding asset** (Faber wiki itself, skill files, decision frameworks)
- **Data flywheel** (per-user longitudinal models, accumulated context)

Unacceptable answers:
- "Our product is just better."
- "We have a head start."
- "Our team is stronger."

This is the **anti-vibe-coding filter**. Build only when the moat is structurally not-software.
