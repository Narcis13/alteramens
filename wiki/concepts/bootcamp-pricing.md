# Bootcamp Pricing — Time-Bounded Packages Beat Perpetual SaaS

For products that solve a problem with a real deadline (exam, certification, launch, season), pricing as a **time-bounded package** matches buyer mental models better than monthly SaaS subscriptions. The package collapses pricing-decision friction and aligns with how the buyer already thinks about spending.

## The Mismatch That SaaS Pricing Creates

Default SaaS pricing ("20 EUR/month forever") assumes:
- Continuous, indefinite usage
- Buyer evaluates value monthly
- Churn is the enemy
- Lifetime value compounds with retention

But many real problems do not match those assumptions. Exam prep ends on exam day. Wedding planning ends on the wedding. Tax season ends in April. Marathon training ends at the race. For these, monthly billing creates anxiety ("am I still using this enough?") and the buyer mentally amortizes against the deadline anyway.

## The Bootcamp Alternative

Price as a **package with a defined start, defined end, and defined outcome**:

- "Pachet Admitere Carol Davila 2027" — 600-900 EUR for 10 months
- "Wedding planning bootcamp — 6 months to your day" — 1,500 EUR
- "USMLE Step 1 prep — March to August" — 2,000 EUR

Buyers already know the structure. They have paid this shape before (meditation, coaching, prep courses). The price is anchored to that comparable, not to "another SaaS subscription."

## Why It Works

1. **Mental model match** — buyers are not deciding "is this worth 20 EUR this month?" They are deciding "is this worth 700 EUR for the whole prep?" The latter is a one-time decision, not an ongoing one.

2. **Anchor to real alternative** — Romanian parents pay 5-10k EUR for admission meditation. A 700 EUR bootcamp is an obvious comparison. A 20 EUR/month SaaS is not.

3. **Cash up front** — package pricing collects revenue at enrollment, not over months. This dramatically improves cash flow vs SaaS.

4. **Aligned outcome** — the package ends when the outcome is decided. Success or failure is measurable. Testimonials become real.

5. **Filters serious users** — committing to a package is a stronger signal of intent than a 1-month subscription. Bootcamp pricing self-selects for committed buyers, raising completion rates and word-of-mouth.

6. **Removes churn anxiety** — there is no churn metric to optimize. Either the cohort delivered the outcome or it did not.

## Variant: Success-Based Pricing

For high-confidence outcomes, split the package into upfront + success fee:

- **400 EUR upfront + 400 EUR if admitted in top 600**

This creates:
- Maximum trust signal (the founder bets on the outcome)
- Stronger filter (only serious users sign up)
- Marketing material with proof (testimonials with numerical outcomes)
- A pricing premium possible because of the trust signal

The risk: success-based pricing requires the founder to have honest confidence in the conversion. Without that confidence the upside is not worth the operational complexity.

## Cliff Business Reality

Bootcamp pricing for a deadline-driven market creates a **cliff business**: revenue concentrates around the enrollment window, not spread evenly across the year. Cash flow planning, hiring, and content production must respect the cliff. A "20 EUR/month" SaaS that grosses 30k/year and a "700 EUR/cohort" bootcamp that grosses 350k/year look different on a chart, even at similar customer count. The bootcamp founder must hold the cash and budget against the next enrollment window.

## When Bootcamp Pricing Does NOT Fit

- Tools used continuously without a deadline (CRM, accounting, monitoring)
- B2B infrastructure where consumption scales over time
- Anything where the value is "always on" rather than "by date X"

For these, traditional SaaS or usage-based pricing remains correct.

## Application: AI Tutor for Medical Admission

See [[brainstorm-ai-tutor-medicina]]. The default pricing model is a 600-900 EUR package for the 10-month run-up to admission. A success-fee variant (400 + 400) is on the table as a trust-signal play. Credit-based usage is reserved as a fallback for marginal users (e.g., a candidate who only wants biochemistry review), not the primary product.
